about

Reliable by design

I build the systems behind streaming platforms — the APIs, subscription lifecycles, payment flows, and background pipelines that have to work every single time someone presses play.

Three things shape how I work: event-driven architecture (RabbitMQ workflows that keep services decoupled and fault-tolerant), observability (Prometheus metrics aggregated across clustered Node.js processes, visualized in Grafana — because you can't fix what you can't see), and performance engineering — caching, batching, and indexing applied where they actually move the numbers.

What I enjoy most is the intersection of building and firefighting: shipping a feature end to end, then when something breaks in production, tracing it to the root cause and fixing it permanently — not patching around it. Beyond code, I mentor interns and junior developers, lead code reviews, and collaborate closely with frontend, QA, and DevOps teams through every release.

User Management

revenue

Subscription Management

Full lifecycles — plans, TVOD, bundles, renewals, cancellations — built to never lose a paying customer to a bug.

+
problem

A subscription is a state machine — purchase, renewal, upgrade, cancellation — and its state lives in two places: the payment provider and our database. Missed events mean drift: lost revenue or wrongly blocked users.

responsibility

Built and maintained the subscription lifecycle services, including TVOD, bundles, and multi-item purchases.

technical decisions

Made verified payment events the source of truth — subscription state changes only in response to events the provider confirmed, flowing through RabbitMQ consumers so the sync survives service restarts.

outcome

Renewals, upgrades, and cancellations reflect what the provider actually recorded — drift stopped being a class of bug.

payments

Payment Integrations

Stripe, PayPal, Razorpay, plus in-app purchases on Android, iOS, and Roku — each with its own webhooks and edge cases.

+
problem

Six payment paths, each with different APIs, webhook formats, retry behavior, and sandbox quirks. This is money — correctness beats speed, always.

responsibility

Owned the gateway integrations and high-availability payment workflows.

technical decisions

Normalized every provider behind a common payment layer so downstream logic never knows which gateway ran. Verified every webhook and made duplicate or out-of-order deliveries safe to process. Verified IAP receipts server-side against store APIs — never trusting the client.

outcome

Reliable checkout across 6+ payment paths. The rule I build by: assume every webhook arrives twice, and make that boring.

growth

Coupon & Promotion Systems

Discount engines with validation rules, redemption limits, and expiry handling, wired into checkout.

+
problem

Marketing needs to run promotions freely — but a validation gap in a discount system leaks revenue silently, one redemption at a time.

responsibility

Built the coupon validation engine and its integration with checkout and subscription pricing.

technical decisions

Centralized rule evaluation at redemption time — eligibility, usage limits, expiry — validated before payment is captured, so an invalid coupon can never produce a valid charge.

outcome

Promotions run without engineering supervision, and double redemptions stopped being possible rather than merely unlikely.

ecosystem

Third-party Integrations

Karix SMS/OTP, AWS S3 uploads, Cloudflare Workers, and external content services behind clean internal APIs.

+
problem

The platform depends on external vendors — messaging, storage, edge compute — each with its own failure modes and rate limits. Coupling business logic directly to vendors makes every outage theirs and ours.

responsibility

Integrated and maintained the third-party service layer.

technical decisions

Wrapped each vendor behind a clean internal API. For media, used S3 signed URLs so large uploads go client-to-S3 directly — authorized by us, but never passing through our API servers. Ran scheduled automation on Cron jobs and Cloudflare Workers.

outcome

Uploads scale with S3 instead of our servers, and vendor failures stay contained behind one boundary.

Debugging live incidents, root cause analysis, and permanent fixes — turning 2 AM alerts into stability.

+
problem

Incidents arrive as error spikes, customer reports, or a metric drifting the wrong way. Restart-and-hope makes them recur; untraced symptoms always come back.

responsibility

production story

The investigation behind the 30%

the symptom

As the content catalog and user base grew, our highest-traffic endpoints — home feed, content listings, playback metadata — were getting noticeably slower. No single change caused it; performance had eroded gradually, which is the hardest kind of problem to see.

the investigation

Instead of guessing, I profiled where the time actually went: slow-query analysis on MongoDB and a close look at what our GraphQL resolvers were doing per request. The data told a clear story — resolvers were triggering N+1 database lookups (one query per item in a list instead of one query for the list), several frequent query patterns had no supporting indexes, and data that rarely changed was being recomputed on every single request.

the fix, in deliberate order

First, DataLoader to batch and de-duplicate resolver lookups — collapsing N+1 patterns into single batched queries. Second, compound MongoDB indexes matched to the real query patterns, plus pagination on unbounded list endpoints. Only then, Redis caching with TTL-based invalidation for hot, slow-changing data. Every step shipped incrementally against live traffic and was measured before moving to the next.

the result

Roughly 30% lower response times on the optimized endpoints and significantly reduced database load — visible immediately in our Prometheus metrics and Grafana dashboards.

what it taught me

The instinct is to reach for a cache first. The data said otherwise: batching and indexing fixed more than caching alone, and caching added last — with a clear invalidation strategy — is the difference between a faster system and a subtly incorrect one. Measure first. Always.
